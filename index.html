Half of all litter on European beaches is single-use plastic: EU bans most commonly found items

29.06.2021

As of July 3rd, single-use plastic items such as straws, cotton bud sticks, and certain food containers are no longer allowed to be sold in the EU. The ban is one measure of the so-called Single-Use Plastics (SUP) Directive. It aims to prevent and reduce the impact of certain plastic products on the environment and on human health.

How were the new laws decided?

Single-use plastics (SUPs) make up 50 per cent of all litter found on European beaches. To plan the directive, the European Commission commissioned a study that used litter data from research projects, monitoring programmes and clean-ups on 276 European beaches in 17 countries. It found that half of all litter on these beaches in 2016 derived from SUP items.

What makes an item single-use?

Single-use plastics (SUPs) are produced to be used once, from a few seconds to a few minutes. SUPs include items like food wrappers, take-away containers, coffee cups and plastic bottles. Because they are used for such a short time, SUPs are more likely to be littered.

The EU study also reported that around 90 per cent of all single-use plastics found on European beaches consist of just 10 different types of items. As a result, the EU decided to target these specific items in its SUP directive.

The directive bans those of the top 10 SUP items for which there is an accessible innovative alternative, no matter whether it is a reusable product, such as a glass straw, or a single-use product made of another material other than plastic, such as paper cotton bud sticks.


The items highlighted in color will be banned: Cotton bud sticks, cutlery, straws, stirrers, balloon sticks, as well as cups and food containers made of expanded polystyrene.

For other SUPs the EU did not consider environmentally-friendly alternatives advanced enough for a market restriction. Examples are plastic bottles, sweets wrappers or cigarette filters. Hence, not all single-use plastic cups and food containers will be banned, just those made of expanded polystyrene. The material is being targeted specifically because it easily breaks down into tiny white plastic balls which are blown around by the wind and eaten by fish or birds who think itâ€™s food.

For the top 10 SUP items for which alternatives are not as developed yet, the directive makes other requirements. To make consumers more aware of which products contain plastics and how to dispose of them correctly, period products, wet wipes, tobacco products and cups will need to have warning labels.

All 27 EU member states are required to enforce the new guidelines. Any countries who don't respect these obligations will be fined. Norway, despite not being a member of the EU, is also implementing the SUP directive as a member of the European Economic Area.

Despite the ban, current SUPs in stock will still be up for sale in Europe to avoid masses of items being discarded without ever having been in use.
